# Section 3.2 Draft: SVO Conditioning

We evaluate whether SVO-conditioned menu scoring changes the Bayesian teacher's behavior on the 150-dialogue held-out set. A reviewer-sensitive detail is that our implementation uses an additive score, `U_self + lambda * E[U_opp]`, rather than a convex interpolation such as `lambda * U_self + (1 - lambda) * E[U_opp]`. In a convex formulation, lambda is bounded and directly describes a tradeoff along the self-other utility segment: moving lambda changes which side of the segment receives more weight. This is closer to the usual interpretation of SVO angle measures, which summarize a person's relative orientation toward self and other outcomes.[^murphy2011] In our additive planner, lambda instead acts as an exchange rate that converts expected opponent utility into self-utility units. This lets `lambda > 1` serve as an altruistic boundary condition, but it also means numerical SVO lambdas are scale-dependent: small values can be overwhelmed by 3-5 point self-utility increments, while large values can saturate into full concession. We therefore compare two regimes: a moderate rescaled mapping (`proself=0.2`, `prosocial=0.6`) and the original boundary mapping (`proself=1.0`, `prosocial=2.0`). For each mapping, we also run a swapped-lambda counterfactual in which proself and prosocial participants receive the opposite lambda. This makes the match-vs-mismatch comparison a direct intervention rather than a post-hoc subgroup split.

Under the rescaled mapping, 84 of 87 accept-eligible turns received a different lambda in the matched vs. mismatched runs, yet the binary accept prediction was identical on every one (0 of 84 flipped), yielding identical Accept-F1 (0.768 in both runs); the per-record correctness vectors are bit-identical, making a Welch test degenerate. Under the legacy boundary mapping, 84 turns changed lambda and 9 changed accept prediction, with a small non-significant Accept-F1 difference (0.911 matched vs. 0.904 mismatched, delta=0.007, Welch p=0.840). We therefore do not claim that SVO matching improves accept decisions in either regime, and we describe the mechanism only as "accept-decision insensitivity to SVO-conditioned lambda" without committing to a specific cause.

The allocation results show the complementary half of the decomposition: allocations are highly sensitive to lambda scale even when accept decisions are not. In the legacy IP-tercile table, proself low-IP agent self-points are `13.510`, while the rescaled overall agent self-points rise to `35.202`; the corresponding human self-points remain near `18` (`17.696` in proself low-IP and `18.113` overall). The legacy boundary mapping also produces a clear saturation failure for prosocial agents: prosocial self-points collapse to `0.000` in every IP tercile (low, mid, and high), while human prosocial self-points remain `16.250`, `18.320`, and `18.533`. The rescaled mapping avoids full-concession collapse and preserves joint-points near the human baseline overall (`36.505` agent vs. `36.493` human), but it over-prioritizes self-points (`35.202` agent vs. `18.113` human). Thus the strongest supported claim is not that SVO matching improves accept decisions. It is that SVO-as-lambda under additive scoring decomposes two effects: binary accept decisions are insensitive within a fixed lambda regime, while allocation behavior is sensitive to the absolute scale of lambda.

Paper-facing takeaway: SVO-conditioned lambda changes allocation behavior, while accept decisions are insensitive to SVO-conditioned lambda under the current policy. Section 3.2 should lead with behavioral-fidelity diagnostics and lambda-saturation limits, while reporting the pre-registered SVO-match accept prediction as a null result.

[^murphy2011]: Murphy et al. (2011) define SVO from allocation choices as a social-preference angle over own and other's outcomes; this supports a relative self-other interpretation, but it does not by itself fix the numerical scale of an additive planner's lambda.
