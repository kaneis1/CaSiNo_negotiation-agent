# Section 3.2 Draft: SVO Conditioning

We evaluate whether SVO-conditioned menu scoring changes the Bayesian teacher's behavior on the 150-dialogue held-out set. The menu score is additive, `U_self + lambda * E[U_opp]`, so we compare two mappings: a moderate rescaled mapping (`proself=0.2`, `prosocial=0.6`) and the original boundary mapping (`proself=1.0`, `prosocial=2.0`). For each mapping, we also run a swapped-lambda counterfactual in which proself and prosocial participants receive the opposite lambda. This makes the match-vs-mismatch comparison a direct intervention rather than a post-hoc subgroup split.

Under the rescaled mapping, 84 of 87 accept-eligible turns received a different lambda in the matched vs. mismatched runs, yet the binary accept prediction was identical on every one (0 of 84 flipped), yielding identical Accept-F1 (0.768 in both runs); the per-record correctness vectors are bit-identical, making a Welch test degenerate. Under the legacy boundary mapping, 84 turns changed lambda and 9 changed accept prediction, with a small non-significant Accept-F1 difference (0.911 matched vs. 0.904 mismatched, delta=0.007, Welch p=0.840). We therefore do not claim that SVO matching improves accept decisions in either regime, and we describe the mechanism only as "accept-decision insensitivity to SVO-conditioned lambda" without committing to a specific cause.

The SVO result is better framed as a sensitivity and saturation analysis. Moderate rescaled lambdas preserve joint-points near the human baseline overall (`36.505` agent vs. `36.493` human), but they over-prioritize self-points (`35.202` agent vs. `18.113` human), indicating that `lambda <= 0.6` is too weak to overcome self-utility increments in the additive planner. The legacy boundary mapping demonstrates the opposite failure mode: proself rows are behaviorally plausible (`17.504` agent self-points vs. `18.417` human), but prosocial rows collapse to full concession (`0.000` agent self-points). This supports a methods claim about lambda scale sensitivity, not a positive matched-SVO accept-F1 claim.

Paper-facing takeaway: SVO-conditioned lambda changes allocation behavior, while accept decisions are insensitive to SVO-conditioned lambda under the current policy. Section 3.2 should lead with behavioral-fidelity diagnostics and lambda-saturation limits, while reporting the pre-registered SVO-match accept prediction as a null result.
