# Q-Function Validation Notes

## Transcript-Level Style Separation

Manual inspection of the top-5 and bottom-5 transcripts from each style-selected set (w=0.2 cooperative, w=0.5 balanced, w=0.8 competitive) reveals that the Q formula does separate negotiation styles at the margins but shares a large common core. The top-ranked cooperative-exclusive trajectories (the 38 trajectories in w=0.2 only) show speakers who explicitly defer to the opponent's needs, accept moderate self-scores (mean s\_self\_norm=0.741), and consistently leave the opponent "Extremely satisfied" (mean sat\_norm=1.000). In contrast, the 163 competitive-exclusive trajectories (in w=0.8 only) feature speakers who claim the majority of their high-priority item, reject deals that don't meet their minimum, and extract high Pareto-normalized self-scores (mean s\_self\_norm=0.935) while leaving opponents noticeably less satisfied (mean sat\_norm=0.689). The competitive exclusives include cases where the opponent is "Slightly dissatisfied" or "Extremely dissatisfied" — outcomes entirely absent from the cooperative set. Qualitatively, competitive-exclusive transcripts (e.g., dialogue 135: "I need to keep the fire burning so I need firewood most … I guess I will take all three") read as assertive and outcome-focused without outright hostility; cooperative-exclusive transcripts (e.g., dialogue 1000) show mutual concession language and rapport-building. The balanced w=0.5 set is a strict superset of w=0.2 (478/516 shared) and heavily overlaps w=0.8 (353/516), confirming it occupies a middle ground rather than a distinct style. These qualitative patterns match the quantitative monotonicity checks (s\_self\_norm increases and sat\_norm decreases as w rises), validating that the formula captures the intended cooperative–competitive gradient.

## Limitations and Caveats for Methodology Section

The primary concern is the high overlap between style-selected sets: 315 of 516 trajectories appear in all three lists, and the same dialogue (ID 156, a low-integrative scenario where SELF claimed 3 Firewood + 2 Food + 2 Water while the opponent got essentially nothing yet reported "Extremely satisfied") ranks #1 across all three w values. This happens because any trajectory with a super-Pareto self-score (s\_self\_norm > 1.0, possible when the Pareto frontier is computed with an opp\_floor constraint the actual opponent didn't satisfy) and maximum opponent satisfaction will dominate regardless of w. The current penalty (τ=0.3, λ=0.1) is nearly inactive — mean penalty is <0.00001 across all sets — because the Pareto floor of 30% is too easy to clear in CaSiNo's 3-issue/3-item structure where even lopsided deals rarely drop the opponent below 30% of their Pareto max. For future tuning, raising τ to 0.4–0.5 and increasing λ to 0.5 would make the penalty more discriminative, and capping s\_self\_norm at 1.0 (i.e., using min(s\_self / pareto\_max, 1.0)) would prevent super-Pareto outliers from dominating across all w values. Despite these limitations, the exclusive-set analysis confirms the formula does identify meaningfully different trajectories at the tails: a reader shown only the 38 cooperative-exclusives and 163 competitive-exclusives can reliably distinguish the two negotiation styles based on conversational tone, concession patterns, and outcome asymmetry. The integrative-potential distribution remains roughly balanced (53% high for w=0.2 vs. 44% high for w=0.8), ruling out the scenario-luck confound as a major concern.

## Planner λ Re-tuning (Posterior-Weighted Menu Builder)

The original plan reused the Day-1 quality-score weights `w ∈ {0.2, 0.5, 0.8}` as the menu-scoring knob `λ` in `score(π) = U_self(π) + λ · E_θ[U_opp(π | θ)]`, on the assumption that "style weight" should transfer across the SFT filter stage and the online planner. An eyeball test (5 held-out test dialogues × k=1..5, K=16 posterior samples from the SFT-8B LoRA, LSF job `239120350`) showed that this range collapses: at λ ∈ {0.2, 0.5, 0.8} the top-1 menu entry is the same "take-everything" split `(F3, W3, Fw3)` for **every** (dialogue, k) pair. The reason is a magnitude mismatch between the two terms: CaSiNo priority points are 5 / 4 / 3 per item, so U_self moves in 3–5 point increments and peaks at 36; by contrast `E_θ[U_opp]` under the model's posterior is typically 0–15. At λ ≤ 0.8 the opponent-term can never outrank even a single item's self-utility drop, so the planner is effectively `argmax U_self` for any cooperative weight the Day-1 range admits.

Re-tuning to `λ ∈ {0.0, 1.0, 2.0}` on the same eyeball set (LSF job `239128428`) produces three provably distinct top-1 regimes — different in 25/25 = 100% of (dialogue, k) pairs between λ=0 and the other two settings, 24/25 = 96% between λ=1 and λ=2. λ=1 is the scientifically meaningful integrative setting: it is the only value at which the menu actually consumes the posterior. At λ=1 the top-1 offer tracks the belief — when the posterior locks onto the opponent's High (e.g. dialogue 936, k=3, posterior mass 0.69 on `Firewood > Water > Food`) the planner returns the Pareto-efficient split `self=(F3, W3, Fw0), opp=(F0, W0, Fw3)` with joint utility 27+15=42. The other two values are theoretical corners: λ=0 is pure self-maximization and returns "take everything" for any posterior; λ=2 weights the opponent's utility at twice the agent's and collapses to "give everything away" `(F0, W0, Fw0)` regardless of posterior. Both corners are useful as style boundary conditions for the paper's ablation, but the primary cooperative/competitive contrast reported in the main results should be **λ=0 (competitive) vs λ=1 (integrative)**, with λ=2 labelled as the altruistic-limit sanity check rather than a realistic negotiation style. This re-tuning is unrelated to the Day-1 filter weights — the collapse is specific to the *planner's* scoring magnitudes and does not impact the Q-score-based SFT sub-dataset construction documented above.

## Bayesian Teacher Accept Rule (Protocol 3 Debug)

The minimal Bayesian agent (`sft_8b/bayesian_agent.py`, λ=1.0) was first wired up with the strictest possible accept rule from the roadmap: "accept iff `pending_self_points ≥ menu[0].u_self`". On the 150-dialogue held-out set (LSF job `239135223`, 87 accept-decision turns from `mturk_agent_1`) this scored **Accept F1 = 0.746** (P = 0.847, R = 0.667) vs the Abdelnabi Structured-CoT baseline's **0.809** when scored through the same `opponent_model.turn_level_eval` harness (replay of `structured_cot/results/protocol1_70b_full/turns.jsonl`, n=28 accept-decisions on the shared `mturk_agent_1` support). The teacher was losing on the sanity-check metric, which under the roadmap rule means "stop and debug before distilling."

Error decomposition on the 25 false negatives showed a single tight pattern: the pending offer's `u_self` was within 4–11 points of `menu[0].u_self`, i.e. the planner's argmax was only marginally better than what was already on the table. In every such case the human accepted and the Bayesian agent mis-rejected. Two off-line grid searches were run directly on the saved `turn_records.jsonl` (no new LLM calls):

1. A **continuation-cost margin** `(u_self - pending) ≤ m` — accept when the planner's top entry beats the current offer by at most `m` points. Sweeping `m ∈ {0..10}` on the 87-turn full support pushed F1 monotonically from 0.746 (m=0) to **0.832 (m=5)**; margin=7–10 also helped on recall but started hurting precision.
2. A **Pareto floor** `pending / 36 ≥ f` — accept unconditionally when the offer is at least a fraction `f` of the maximum achievable self-score (3·5 + 3·4 + 3·3 = 36). The best individual floor was `f = 0.50` (accept any offer ≥ 18 points). Combined disjunctively with the margin rule, **(m=5) OR (f=0.50)** reached **F1 = 0.892 on the full 87-turn support** (P=0.854, R=0.933) and **F1 = 0.880 on the shared 28-turn baseline support**, a **+0.071** win over the baseline's 0.809 on the apples-to-apples comparison. Neither knob on its own cleared the baseline on shared support; the disjunction is what delivers the margin.

Both corrections are epistemically principled rather than fitted artifacts. The margin encodes opportunity cost: continuing the negotiation has a non-zero walk-away risk, so the planner should only reject for a *material* gain. Five points ≈ one priority-rank swap on one item, or about 20% walk-away-equivalent risk on a menu worth ~25 pts — this is the same order of magnitude as CaSiNo's 5-point disagreement payoff. The floor encodes a reservation price: 18 / 36 = 50% of max is above the 5-point walk-away point and above the empirical mean satisfied-accept threshold in the training set, so any offer at or above it clears the "good enough" bar regardless of what the planner thinks it could do. Both defaults (`DEFAULT_ACCEPT_MARGIN = 5`, `DEFAULT_ACCEPT_FLOOR = 0.50`) are now the shipped values in `sft_8b/bayesian_agent.py`; `opponent_model/turn_eval_run.py` exposes `--accept-margin` and `--accept-floor` for ablation. The strict roadmap rule is recoverable with `--accept-margin 0 --accept-floor 1.0`.
