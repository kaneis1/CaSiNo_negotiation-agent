# Q-Function Validation Notes

## Transcript-Level Style Separation

Manual inspection of the top-5 and bottom-5 transcripts from each style-selected set (w=0.2 cooperative, w=0.5 balanced, w=0.8 competitive) reveals that the Q formula does separate negotiation styles at the margins but shares a large common core. The top-ranked cooperative-exclusive trajectories (the 38 trajectories in w=0.2 only) show speakers who explicitly defer to the opponent's needs, accept moderate self-scores (mean s\_self\_norm=0.741), and consistently leave the opponent "Extremely satisfied" (mean sat\_norm=1.000). In contrast, the 163 competitive-exclusive trajectories (in w=0.8 only) feature speakers who claim the majority of their high-priority item, reject deals that don't meet their minimum, and extract high Pareto-normalized self-scores (mean s\_self\_norm=0.935) while leaving opponents noticeably less satisfied (mean sat\_norm=0.689). The competitive exclusives include cases where the opponent is "Slightly dissatisfied" or "Extremely dissatisfied" — outcomes entirely absent from the cooperative set. Qualitatively, competitive-exclusive transcripts (e.g., dialogue 135: "I need to keep the fire burning so I need firewood most … I guess I will take all three") read as assertive and outcome-focused without outright hostility; cooperative-exclusive transcripts (e.g., dialogue 1000) show mutual concession language and rapport-building. The balanced w=0.5 set is a strict superset of w=0.2 (478/516 shared) and heavily overlaps w=0.8 (353/516), confirming it occupies a middle ground rather than a distinct style. These qualitative patterns match the quantitative monotonicity checks (s\_self\_norm increases and sat\_norm decreases as w rises), validating that the formula captures the intended cooperative–competitive gradient.

## Limitations and Caveats for Methodology Section

The primary concern is the high overlap between style-selected sets: 315 of 516 trajectories appear in all three lists, and the same dialogue (ID 156, a low-integrative scenario where SELF claimed 3 Firewood + 2 Food + 2 Water while the opponent got essentially nothing yet reported "Extremely satisfied") ranks #1 across all three w values. This happens because any trajectory with a super-Pareto self-score (s\_self\_norm > 1.0, possible when the Pareto frontier is computed with an opp\_floor constraint the actual opponent didn't satisfy) and maximum opponent satisfaction will dominate regardless of w. The current penalty (τ=0.3, λ=0.1) is nearly inactive — mean penalty is <0.00001 across all sets — because the Pareto floor of 30% is too easy to clear in CaSiNo's 3-issue/3-item structure where even lopsided deals rarely drop the opponent below 30% of their Pareto max. For future tuning, raising τ to 0.4–0.5 and increasing λ to 0.5 would make the penalty more discriminative, and capping s\_self\_norm at 1.0 (i.e., using min(s\_self / pareto\_max, 1.0)) would prevent super-Pareto outliers from dominating across all w values. Despite these limitations, the exclusive-set analysis confirms the formula does identify meaningfully different trajectories at the tails: a reader shown only the 38 cooperative-exclusives and 163 competitive-exclusives can reliably distinguish the two negotiation styles based on conversational tone, concession patterns, and outcome asymmetry. The integrative-potential distribution remains roughly balanced (53% high for w=0.2 vs. 44% high for w=0.8), ruling out the scenario-luck confound as a major concern.
